What are Zombie Sciences?


"The ultimate mark of power may be its invisibility; the ultimate challenge, the exposition of its roots.” - Michel-Rolph Trouillot, Silencing the Past: Power and the Production of History


"Organizers campaigning against carceral technologies are not organizing against ‘intense creepiness.’ They are organizing against a category of violence...that has a long history of racialized surveillance, and a short history of digital surveillance." - Sarah T. Hamid, Carceral Tech Resistance Network (CTRN)



Year after year, century after century, violent and nonsensical scientific ideas are cut down and put to rest—only to return deadlier than ever and with alluring new names. Nazis called it eugenics, but today scientists call it sociogenomics. Colonial anthropologists called it craniology and gentleman scientists called it physiognomy, but today’s hottest research papers call it biometrics. Researchers of yore claimed that violent behavior stemmed from extra-large Y chromosomes, but today’s computer scientists say it’s encoded in faces and voices.

These zombie sciences, old and new, are cut from the same sullied cloth. Despite changes in terminology and methods, zombie sciences are all predicated on the construction of people as inherently inferior, undesirable, dangerous, and disposable. On top of these claims, members of dominant groups build technologies to capture and control these “deviant” and “delinquent” classes.

The ghoulish remnants of this violent past lingers on in today’s slick interfaces and algorithms. These bloody histories are often sanitized through a technocratic rhetoric which now frames machine learning and “big data” as innovative and cutting-edge, objective, and rigorous. But it’s the same deadly shit—reanimated, haggard, and back to haunt us.

Our timeline traces the legacy of these grotesque ideas–and the acts carried out in their name–revealing how carceral biometrics (e.g. physiognomy, phrenology) and the sciences of social control (e.g. behaviorism, eugenics) are alive and well today. They are the lifelines that keep undead ideas alive.



The Undead Horde of Biometrics



On the Natural Varieties of Mankind (3rd ed.) – Blumenbach

Blumenbach proposed that there were five fundamentally distinct variants of human beings, all distinguishable by skull shape and size. He believed that Caucasians (white Europeans) were the “original” race of the earliest humans on earth and that all other racial groups could reverse their “degeneration” and become Caucasian through proper environmental control, a notion that rationalized white supremacy and gave settler colonial and imperial projects a benevolent veneer.

Crania America - Morton

Settlers of the Americas used books like Morton’s Crania America to justify the removal of Native people from their land in the 1830’s and 40’s. Morton’s study of skulls was cited in various articles targeted at white settlers moving out west. One article stated that Native people were “adverse to cultivation, slow in acquiring knowledge” and thus not conducive to industrialization and “progress.” This research helped to justify Andrew Jackson’s forceful relocation of 60,000 Indigenous peoples in a series of death marches commonly known as the “Trail of Tears.”

Popular Phrenology - Coombs

By the mid-19th century phrenology had been debunked as a scientific enterprise, but it was still steadily on the rise in popular culture. Phrenological manuals on marriage proved incredibly popular. As Coombs argued: “One of the first requisites in a good wife is to ascertain that she has a good head.” Particular attention was paid to the phrenological indicators of child-rearing and sexuality.

Somatotypes - Sheldon

William Herbert Sheldon developed a theory of “somatotypes,” which were a set of general body shapes that could be used as indicators of intelligence, personality, or moral character. Sheldeon developed these typologies by relying on nude photos of freshman college students which were taken under the guise of “posture correction,” which he used to create theories about how to predict a person’s potential for criminality or leadership.

The Diversity of Origin of the Human Race

Louis Agassiz, a Swiss natural scientist at Harvard University, photographed the bodies of several African-born enslaved men and women on South Carolina plantations (including Renty, the ancestor of Tamara Lanier https://www.harvardfreerenty.com/) with the goal of validating theories of polygenism. These theories posit that there are multiple origins to the human race and that Black bodies are genetically distinct and inferior, which Agassiz and his supporters use to justify chattel slavery.

Report on the Diseases and Physical Peculiarities of the Negro Race - Cartwright

Cartwright coined the term “drapetomania” or, “the disease causing Negros to run away,” to justify and naturalize slavery, characterizing enslaved people's strivings for liberation as an unnatural, pathological mental condition.

The Criminal Man - Lombroso

Lombroso argued that criminal propensity could be inferred through analyses of bodily form, catalyzing a revival of interest in phrenology and forming the foundation of the field of criminology. Despite being widely debunked, contemporary biometric research that aims to “predict criminality” regularly references Lombroso's ideas, i.e. does someone have “the face of a criminal.”

Physiognomy Illustrated - Simms

This text provides a visual atlas of how to read facial characteristics, bodily, and skull structure for a person's moral character, intellectual faculties, and inclinations toward good or evil, a practice known as physiognomy (of which phrenology was a subset). The underlying assumption is that certain kinds of features represent qualitatively distinct types or categories of people--like the "superlatively scientific" face of race scientist J.F. Blumenbach. This work has been used to justify the hierarchical ranking of humans according to race, gender, ability, class, etc.

Practical Character Reader - Vaught

The Practical Character Reader mapped out personality traits like "genuine" and "unreliable" to specific skull shapes. Similar claims are echoed in contemporary facial recognition papers that seek to identify "genuine" and "trustworthy" people based on their facial measurements.

Behavioral Supervision System - Schwitzgebel

Schwitzgebel defined behavioral engineering as "the application of electromechanical technology to the understanding, maintenance, and modification of human behavior," expanding Skinnerian behaviorism with an eye toward practical and policy-level applications. He encouraged the use of portable electric shock devices to suppress “deviant” behaviors such as smoking, nail biting, homosexuality and drug addiction. The aim of this work was to course correct people’s in-born deficiencies through “observing and possibly controlling behavior at a distance.”

Facial Expressions of Affect in Autistic, Mentally R******* and Normal Children

While not the first or last paper of its kind, this popular study uses facial expressions to identify cognitive difference. The authors argue that the purported ambiguity of neurodivergent children’s expressions is indicative of their “infrequent sharing of attention with their caregivers and other individuals,” and the study constructs autism and intellectual disabilities as visible forms of deviance.

The Bell Curve - Herrnstein and Murray

In The Bell Curve, the authors revived eugenics thinking in educational policy, arguing that group-level differences in IQ were the source of low academic achievement in African-American communities. The authors claimed that evidence pointed ""toward a genetic factor in cognitive ethnic differences,” which drove persistent inequality across racial groups. This line of thinking was used to justify ongoing divestment of educational resources from low income and African American children.

Excess of Counterclockwise Scalp Hair Whorl Rotation in Homosexual Men - Klar

Klar asserts that counter-clockwise hair whorls is a biomarker (a biological indicator) of homosexuality in men, arguing that sexual preference was “dictated by the person’s mental makeup owing to the naturally occurring genetic variation in brain laterality development.” In addition to suggesting that hair patterns might also be a reliable biomarker for autism, schizophrenia, and “cognitive deficiency,” the article solidifies the notion that socially constructed non-normativity is both biologically based and visually identifiable.

Parabon Nanolabs

Parabon Nanolabs develops and sells a forensic analysis service called Snapshot that predicts a person’s physical appearance from their DNA. Law enforcement agencies have used Parabon and companies like it to regularly make new arrests. These services depend heavily on genetic profile databases which often depend on extracting data from incarcerated people (in China, this has been deployed primarily on Uyghur inmates; in the US, there are two programs that take DNA samples from immigrant detainees and asylum seekers).

The Accuracy of Inferences About Criminality Based on Facial Appearance - Valla et al

This paper echoes Lombroso’s work from the late 19th century by claiming that criminal propensity is detectable through close observation of the face. The authors assert that their research subjects were able to distinguish criminals from non-criminals based on photos alone, even after controlling for things like gender, race, age, attractiveness, and emotional displays. By normalizing “what a criminal looks like,” this study only re-entrenches many existing biases that connect physical difference with danger and moral character.

The Anatomy of Violence: The Biological Roots of Crime - Raine

The Anatomy of Violence is a contemporary example of physiognomy, based on the idea that violent and aggressive behavior is rooted in abnormal physiological functioning. The author claims that brain-imaging techniques can be used to identify physical deformations and functional abnormalities that predispose people to violence.

Criminal Faces - Wu and Zhang

The authors of this since-retracted study developed an image classifier to differentiate the faces of “criminals from the law abiding public,” based only on still images. While the authors claimed their aim was simply to “extend the research on automated face recognition from the biometric dimension to the sociopsychological dimension,” it faced significant criticism upon publication, with many people calling the study neo-phrenological.

Faceception

Faceception is a company that claims to have built a computer vision algorithm that can “detect anonymous threats” (i.e. potential murderers, pedophiles and terrorists) in public spaces through automated analysis of facial features. The company pitches their product as a public safety tool for doing real-time crime analysis in critical infrastructure like mass transit, airports, public parks and stadiums. This work is only one of many recent reincarnations of physiognomy, haunting the hottest AI start-ups coming out of Silicon Valley and beyond.

Digital pills

FDA approved first “digital pill,” which adds sensors activated by stomach fluid to pills and couples, with a skin patch, with a Bluetooth app in order to monitor whether or not a person is taking medication as prescribed. Concerns have already arose that digital pills could become a requirement for probation or release from involuntary hospitalization, which is magnified by the already-prevalent use of psychotropic drugs that have been used to control inmates across the US prison, military, and welfare systems. Abilify, an antipsychotic, is the first drug approved as a digital pill.

Gay Faces - Wang and Kosinski

In 2018, two researchers created a deep neural network that they claimed could predict a person's sexuality (either gay or straight – clearly the only two options, folks) based on their face. By the look of their composite images, turns out straight guys have goatees and straight women wear eyeliner.

Detecting Personality Traits Based on Eye Tracking Data - Berkovsky et al

This paper proposes a framework for “objective personality detection” using eye-tracking sensors. The authors built a classifier that they claim is capable of accurately predicting a wide range of personality traits such as honesty, narcissism, and psychopathy. Sounds a lot like old physiognomic wine in new machine learning bottles.

Tracking Historical Changes in Trustworthiness Using Machine Learning Analyses of Facial Cues in Paintings - Safra et al

In September 2020, researchers claimed to “automatically generate trustworthiness evaluations,” which they used to argue that social trustworthiness among western Europeans “increased over the period 1500-200 paralleling the decline of interpersonal violence and the rise of democratic values observed in Western Europe… such as religious tolerance, political freedom and democracy.” This dangerously ignorant and racist interpretation of violent European colonialism reminds us that, given enough fancy mathematics, even global genocide can be whitewashed to fit a linear regression.

Student surveillance software

Between Proctorio, ExamSoft, and Respondus LockDown browser, online learning has led to a proliferation of software claiming to prevent cheating. From students of color needing to jump through hoops for their faces to be recognized, to disabled students worried their facial tics will be used as proof of cheating, this software’s main achievements are student mass surveillance and carcerizing the home, disproportionately affecting marginalized people.

Pop culture phrenology

In a throwback to the marriage guides and skull atlases of the 19th century, phrenology has made a comeback in popular culture under the guise of AI. In supermarket tabloids like the U.K. Sun, staff writers have claimed to use an algorithm like the one developed by Safra et al to rate the “trustworthiness” of celebrities, noting that prominent cheekbones and high eyebrows were sure signs of trustworthiness.

Wrongful Arrest of Robert Julian-Borchak Williams

Williams is the first known person to be wrongfully arrested based on an incorrect match from facial recognition technology, demonstrating the harms of using AI to identify “criminal faces.” The Detroit Police Department wrongly identified Williams by running surveillance footage stills through a facial recognition algorithm provided by DataWorkPlus, despite the fact that a federal study revealed the algorithm falsely identified the faces of African-American and Asian people up to 100 times more than the faces of white people.

Rogers and Karabiyak

Researchers recently announced plans to use artificial intelligence (AI) to gather and analyze biometric data such as heart rate and galvanic skin response, all in order to identify and intervene on “risky behaviors, stressful situations and other behavioral and physiological factors that lead toward recidivism.” While the stated goal of the project is to use technology to teach better coping and life skills to prevent people from committing crimes, the project’s focus--intervening on “behavioral and physiological factors correlated with...criminal behavior”--follows a long history of statisticians like Pearson and Galton who aim to minimize “undesirable behavior” by drawing connections between racialized biometrics and criminality.

Neuroprediction and AI in Forensic Psychiatry and Criminal Justice - Tortora et al

This paper explores the possibility of fusing machine learning methods with neuro-imaging data to reveal information about a person’s criminal disposition. Like phrenologists of yore, the researchers claim that antisocial behavioral traits like “lack of empathy” have a neurobiological basis. The authors anticipate new machine learning methods will fuel the development of new criminal risk assessments based on AI “brain readings.”

Neural and Behavioral Correlates of Sacred Values and Vulnerability to Violent Extremism - Petras et al

An fMRI study of "those who expressed willingness to engage in or facilitate, violence associated with jihadist causes" claims to have identified a region of the brain responsible for "sacred value processing", and was widely reported as being able to identify terrorists via brain scan. This is repackaged, Lombroso-esque identification of “The Criminal Man” via supposed physical abnormalities and the conflation of Islam and terrorism, since they conveniently ignore all white-supremacist violence over the past 80 years.

Massachusetts Board of State Charities

In 1866 the Massachusetts Board of State Charities wrote that the chief cause for such a “large proportion of dependent and destructive members of our community” was “ inherited organic imperfection, vitiated constitution or poor stock.” This proclamation is an early expression of the notion that certain classes of people are inherently flawed, deviant, undeserving, and unworthy members of society and ought to, for the betterment of humankind, be disposed of or no longer exist.

Inquiries into Human Faculty and Its Development - Galton

In this text, Galton coined the term “eugenics,” from the Greek prefix eu (“well” or “good”) to describe the idea of using social control (i.e. forced sterilization, housing displacement, behavioral engineering, etc.) to improve the health and intelligence of future generations. The violence, genocide, and oppression carried out in the name of eugenics demonstrates how ostensibly positive-seeming metrics to evaluate things like “health” and “intelligence” can be used to justify violence against stigmatized groups.

The Problem of Practical Eugenics; founder of the journal Annals of Eugenics - Pearson

Through a series of eight books, Pearson lent a veneer of scientificity to eugenics through the development of statistical techniques to measure qualities such as “feeble-mindedness” and “pauperism.” Poverty was generally characterized as a “relative inefficiency” in cognitive and moral functioning.

Indiana passes forced sterilization law

In the United States, a widespread eugenics movement led to the forced sterilization of tens of thousands of people considered “unfit” to reproduce. Indiana passed the first of these laws in 1907. By the end of the 1920s, twenty-four states passed laws permitting the involuntary sterilization of people perceived to be “mentally unfit.”

Eugenics Records Office established

Funded by John D. Rockefeller and the Carnegie Institution, the Eugenics Record Office was founded in 1910 on Long Island. The ERO analysed ranges of physical to tempermantal properites through questionnaires with "a commitment to improve race-betterment." In the US, ERO work (propoganda) led to forced sterilization of individuals with "undesirable" characteristics.

Eugenics and education policy

In the 1920’s a group of psychologists touted IQ tests as a means of justifying unequal educational resources and outcomes. These experts argued that it made no sense to invest in more opportunities for children of lower socioeconomic groups, because those children supposedly did not have the in-born ability to compete. Proponents of IQ testing believed in the primacy of heredity over environment and that the immigration of “inferior” races (anyone not from Scandania or Western Europe) was driving America toward a dysgenic future.

Eugenics in Nazi Germany

The American eugenics movement had considerable influence on Adolf Hitler and the Third Reich, which used the scientific literature to justify the genocidal extermination of Jewish people, people with disabilities, homosexuals, and numerous other “deviant” classes. Germans who were tried for genocide after the war cited California’s eugenic sterilization law as part of their defense. 60,000 people were forcibly sterilized under California’s law. Eugenic policies fell out of favor for some time after the revelation of these horrific crimes against humanity.

Mankind Quarterly founded

Dissatisfied with the scientific community's turn away from (and refusal to publish) eugenics and race sciences, a handful of like-minded researchers, established their own peer-reviewed journal. The founders include the former Nazi scientist Otmar von Verschuer, British eugenicist Roger Pearson, along with Robert Gayre, Henry Garrett, Corrado Gini, and Reginald Ruggles Gates. The articles published in its pages fueled arguments in favor of eugenics policies, while keeping alive the notion of an “undeserving poor” who would never improve or thrive regardless of the resources invested in their wellbeing.

How Much Can We Boost IQ and Scholastic Achievement - Jensen

Eugenics made a comeback in the late 1960's, coinciding with a white backlash against government programs that were intended to close racial gaps in education, health and employment. Jensen’s Harvard Educational Review article argued that compensatory education programs like Project Head Start were no match for the fundamental genetic limitations in poor Black populations, an argument that revived interest in the influence of genetics on intelligence and justified divestment from essential public services.

Length of the Y Chromosome in Criminal Males - Nielsen

During the 20th century, scientists ran genetic tests on men in state institutions (prisons, mental asylums, etc.) and discovered that a number of these men had an extra Y-chromosome. This finding catalyzed theories about the existence of a "criminal gene." Although the correlation between additional Y chromosomes and aggression has never been proven, research like Nielsen’s solidified the image of a genetic mutant super (cis) male, and the cultural imagination surrounding aggression and masculinity.

Relf v. Weinberger

Two Black sisters (12 and 14 years old) in Alabama were sterilized without their consent. With the help of the Southern Poverty Law Center, they sued the US government and drew national attention to the issue of state-sanctioned sterilization in the American South. This case led to mandatory informed consent laws.

Heads, or the Art of Phrenology - Cooper and Cooper

In Heads, Peter Cooper and Helen Cooper give an abridged history of phrenology and give users a framework to apply the “Art” into one’s personal life in order to become successful. As a psychologist, Peter Cooper was purportedly one of the leaders in focus group marketing research in the 1960s, and he projected psychological classifications of human behavior onto the human skull. In the late 1980s, Cooper’s London Phrenology Company commissioned 1000 limited-edition porcelain heads.

Intelligence and the Wealth and Poverty of Nations - Lynn and Vanhanen

This paper presents a dataset that the authors purport can be used to comparatively measure cognitive abilities of entire countries and populations according to IQ test scores, which are highly contested, reductive, racist, and ableist measures of "intelligence." In addition to fundamental flaws with the dataset that make any conclusions drawn from the data patently false, Lynn has suggested that the data justifies that “nations with higher IQs” should subjugate or eliminate “nations with lower IQs.” Many other researchers have used the dataset to make racially-based claims about economic growth and civil wars.

Epigenetic Programming by Maternal Behavior - Weaver et al

The field of epigenetics has renewed notions of “the undeserving poor” and has been used to justify the elimination of programs and policies designed to bridge achievement gaps in education as well as the dehumanizing treatment of people with disabilities. In 2004, neuroscientist Michael Meaney and geneticist Moshe Szyf led a team to study how early life experiences shape a person’s brain and influence physical health and behavior later in life. This paper echoes earlier eugenics thinking by providing a gene-based mechanism for understanding how poverty translates into low educational achievement.

An fMRI study of "those who expressed willingness to engage in or facilitate, violence associated with jihadist causes" claims to have identified a region of the brain responsible for "sacred value processing", and was widely reported as being able to identify terrorists via brain scan. This is repackaged, Lombroso-esque identification of “The Criminal Man” via supposed physical abnormalities and the conflation of Islam and terrorism, since they conveniently ignore all white-supremacist violence over the past 80 years.

Massachusetts Board of State Charities

In 1866 the Massachusetts Board of State Charities wrote that the chief cause for such a “large proportion of dependent and destructive members of our community” was “ inherited organic imperfection, vitiated constitution or poor stock.” This proclamation is an early expression of the notion that certain classes of people are inherently flawed, deviant, undeserving, and unworthy members of society and ought to, for the betterment of humankind, be disposed of or no longer exist.

Inquiries into Human Faculty and Its Development - Galton

In this text, Galton coined the term “eugenics,” from the Greek prefix eu (“well” or “good”) to describe the idea of using social control (i.e. forced sterilization, housing displacement, behavioral engineering, etc.) to improve the health and intelligence of future generations. The violence, genocide, and oppression carried out in the name of eugenics demonstrates how ostensibly positive-seeming metrics to evaluate things like “health” and “intelligence” can be used to justify violence against stigmatized groups.

The Problem of Practical Eugenics; founder of the journal Annals of Eugenics - Pearson

Through a series of eight books, Pearson lent a veneer of scientificity to eugenics through the development of statistical techniques to measure qualities such as “feeble-mindedness” and “pauperism.” Poverty was generally characterized as a “relative inefficiency” in cognitive and moral functioning.

Indiana passes forced sterilization law

In the United States, a widespread eugenics movement led to the forced sterilization of tens of thousands of people considered “unfit” to reproduce. Indiana passed the first of these laws in 1907. By the end of the 1920s, twenty-four states passed laws permitting the involuntary sterilization of people perceived to be “mentally unfit.”

Eugenics Records Office established

Funded by John D. Rockefeller and the Carnegie Institution, the Eugenics Record Office was founded in 1910 on Long Island. The ERO analysed ranges of physical to tempermantal properites through questionnaires with "a commitment to improve race-betterment." In the US, ERO work (propoganda) led to forced sterilization of individuals with "undesirable" characteristics.

Eugenics and education policy

In the 1920’s a group of psychologists touted IQ tests as a means of justifying unequal educational resources and outcomes. These experts argued that it made no sense to invest in more opportunities for children of lower socioeconomic groups, because those children supposedly did not have the in-born ability to compete. Proponents of IQ testing believed in the primacy of heredity over environment and that the immigration of “inferior” races (anyone not from Scandania or Western Europe) was driving America toward a dysgenic future.

Eugenics in Nazi Germany

The American eugenics movement had considerable influence on Adolf Hitler and the Third Reich, which used the scientific literature to justify the genocidal extermination of Jewish people, people with disabilities, homosexuals, and numerous other “deviant” classes. Germans who were tried for genocide after the war cited California’s eugenic sterilization law as part of their defense. 60,000 people were forcibly sterilized under California’s law. Eugenic policies fell out of favor for some time after the revelation of these horrific crimes against humanity.

Mankind Quarterly founded

Dissatisfied with the scientific community's turn away from (and refusal to publish) eugenics and race sciences, a handful of like-minded researchers, established their own peer-reviewed journal. The founders include the former Nazi scientist Otmar von Verschuer, British eugenicist Roger Pearson, along with Robert Gayre, Henry Garrett, Corrado Gini, and Reginald Ruggles Gates. The articles published in its pages fueled arguments in favor of eugenics policies, while keeping alive the notion of an “undeserving poor” who would never improve or thrive regardless of the resources invested in their wellbeing.

Behavioral Supervision System - Schwitzgebel

Schwitzgebel defined behavioral engineering as "the application of electromechanical technology to the understanding, maintenance, and modification of human behavior," expanding Skinnerian behaviorism with an eye toward practical and policy-level applications. He encouraged the use of portable electric shock devices to suppress “deviant” behaviors such as smoking, nail biting, homosexuality and drug addiction. The aim of this work was to course correct people’s in-born deficiencies through “observing and possibly controlling behavior at a distance.”

How Much Can We Boost IQ and Scholastic Achievement - Jensen

Eugenics made a comeback in the late 1960's, coinciding with a white backlash against government programs that were intended to close racial gaps in education, health and employment. Jensen’s Harvard Educational Review article argued that compensatory education programs like Project Head Start were no match for the fundamental genetic limitations in poor Black populations, an argument that revived interest in the influence of genetics on intelligence and justified divestment from essential public services.

Length of the Y Chromosome in Criminal Males - Nielsen

During the 20th century, scientists ran genetic tests on men in state institutions (prisons, mental asylums, etc.) and discovered that a number of these men had an extra Y-chromosome. This finding catalyzed theories about the existence of a "criminal gene." Although the correlation between additional Y chromosomes and aggression has never been proven, research like Nielsen’s solidified the image of a genetic mutant super (cis) male, and the cultural imagination surrounding aggression and masculinity.

Relf v. Weinberger

Two Black sisters (12 and 14 years old) in Alabama were sterilized without their consent. With the help of the Southern Poverty Law Center, they sued the US government and drew national attention to the issue of state-sanctioned sterilization in the American South. This case led to mandatory informed consent laws.

Heads, or the Art of Phrenology - Cooper and Cooper

In Heads, Peter Cooper and Helen Cooper give an abridged history of phrenology and give users a framework to apply the “Art” into one’s personal life in order to become successful. As a psychologist, Peter Cooper was purportedly one of the leaders in focus group marketing research in the 1960s, and he projected psychological classifications of human behavior onto the human skull. In the late 1980s, Cooper’s London Phrenology Company commissioned 1000 limited-edition porcelain heads.

Facial Expressions of Affect in Autistic, Mentally R******* and Normal Children

While not the first or last paper of its kind, this popular study uses facial expressions to identify cognitive difference. The authors argue that the purported ambiguity of neurodivergent children’s expressions is indicative of their “infrequent sharing of attention with their caregivers and other individuals,” and the study constructs autism and intellectual disabilities as visible forms of deviance.

The Bell Curve - Herrnstein and Murray

In The Bell Curve, the authors revived eugenics thinking in educational policy, arguing that group-level differences in IQ were the source of low academic achievement in African-American communities. The authors claimed that evidence pointed ""toward a genetic factor in cognitive ethnic differences,” which drove persistent inequality across racial groups. This line of thinking was used to justify ongoing divestment of educational resources from low income and African American children.

Intelligence and the Wealth and Poverty of Nations - Lynn and Vanhanen

This paper presents a dataset that the authors purport can be used to comparatively measure cognitive abilities of entire countries and populations according to IQ test scores, which are highly contested, reductive, racist, and ableist measures of "intelligence." In addition to fundamental flaws with the dataset that make any conclusions drawn from the data patently false, Lynn has suggested that the data justifies that “nations with higher IQs” should subjugate or eliminate “nations with lower IQs.” Many other researchers have used the dataset to make racially-based claims about economic growth and civil wars.

Epigenetic Programming by Maternal Behavior - Weaver et al

The field of epigenetics has renewed notions of “the undeserving poor” and has been used to justify the elimination of programs and policies designed to bridge achievement gaps in education as well as the dehumanizing treatment of people with disabilities. In 2004, neuroscientist Michael Meaney and geneticist Moshe Szyf led a team to study how early life experiences shape a person’s brain and influence physical health and behavior later in life. This paper echoes earlier eugenics thinking by providing a gene-based mechanism for understanding how poverty translates into low educational achievement.

Digital pills

FDA approved first “digital pill,” which adds sensors activated by stomach fluid to pills and couples, with a skin patch, with a Bluetooth app in order to monitor whether or not a person is taking medication as prescribed. Concerns have already arose that digital pills could become a requirement for probation or release from involuntary hospitalization, which is magnified by the already-prevalent use of psychotropic drugs that have been used to control inmates across the US prison, military, and welfare systems. Abilify, an antipsychotic, is the first drug approved as a digital pill.

safeM8 - Church

Harvard Medical School geneticist George Church is developing safeM8, a dating app that screens out matches that might result in a child with a genetic disease with the goal of eliminating all heritable diseases. In an interview on 60 Minutes, Church argues that eliminating these diseases would save $1 trillion worldwide. However, this eugenic practice functionally argues for the elimination of people who have genetic conditions that are considered “inferior” under the guise of automated “personal preference.”
